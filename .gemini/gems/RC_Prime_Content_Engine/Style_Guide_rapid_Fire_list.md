1. SynergyThe "North Star" of 30 Rock buzzwords. Jack Donaghy defines it as the ultimate goal of any merger, though in practice, it usually just involves Liz Lemon being forced to put a microwave oven in a sketch. It's the quintessential word used to justify doing two things poorly at the same time.

Jack: "Don't fight synergy, Lemon. It's bigger than all of us."

Liz: "But you're just putting GE microwaves on my show!"

Jack: "First of all, never bad-mouth synergy. It’s the ultimate goal of any merger. It’s like the movie The Core, but with profits."

2. Vertical Integration Often used by Jack to describe the logic of a massive conglomerate like GE. It’s the idea that the company should own everything from the satellites that broadcast the signal to the factory that makes the plastic for the remote controls. It mocks the corporate desire for total, suffocating control.

Jack: "Imagine that your favorite corn chip manufacturer also owned the number one diarrhea medication."

Liz: "That’d be great, 'cause then they could put a little sample of the medicine in each bag."

Jack: "Keep thinking."

Liz: "Except then they might be tempted to make the corn chips give you..."

Jack: "Vertical integration."

3. Six SigmaFey mocks this real-world management philosophy by turning it into a pseudo-religious cult. In the show, the "Six Sigmas" are represented by six men who embody the core traits of corporate excellence: Teamwork, Insight, Brutality, Male Enhancement, Hand-shake-fulness, and Play-Hard. 

 Jack: "I'm a Six Sigma Black Belt. I’ve mastered the six pillars of corporate excellence: Teamwork, Insight, Brutality, Male Enhancement, Hand-shake-fulness, and Play-Hard."

Liz: "Wait, is 'Male Enhancement' one of them?"

Jack: "It’s a very masculine philosophy, Lemon."

4. Innoventually A classic Jack Donaghy "innovention." It’s a portmanteau of "innovate," "invent," and "eventually." It mocks the corporate tendency to create nonsense words that sound visionary but actually mean "we will think of something profitable later."

Jack: "It’s an 'innovention'—a word I just innovented. And I guarantee it will innoventually be used in every corporate meeting room from here to the Sheinhardt Wig Company."

5. SubordifriendA term used to describe the "unacceptable" blurring of lines between a boss and an employee. Fey uses this to mock the awkward, forced camaraderie of modern workplace culture where your manager wants to be your "bestie" while also being the person who can fire you.

Jack: "I need a word for our relationship, Lemon. You’re more than a subordinate, but less than a friend. You’re a friend-ployee. Or a subordifriend. Actually, those both sound like things a person in a cult would say before they poisoned the punch."

6. Revenue Streams In the episode "Blind Date," Liz Lemon wins over Jack by simply rattling off a string of buzzwords without context: "Cross-promotional. Deal mechanics. Revenue streams. Jargon. Synergy." Jack’s response? "That’s the best presentation I’ve ever seen. Get started right away."  

Liz: (Panic-pitching to Jack) "Cross-promotional. Deal mechanics. Revenue streams. Jargon. Synergy."

Jack: (Stunned) "That’s the best presentation I’ve ever seen. Get started right away."

7. Reaganing The corporate equivalent of "winning." Jack describes it as a state of being where you can do no wrong—inspired by Ronald Reagan. In business culture, it mocks the "rockstar" CEO mythos where a single person’s charisma is seen as a substitute for actual strategy.

Jack: "I’m Reaganing, Lemon. For twenty-four hours, I have been in a state of perfect decision-making. Like Ronald Reagan. I made a perfect sandwich, I solved the company’s logistics crisis, and I haven't even had to yell at a single immigrant."

8. Business Drunk According to Jack, "Business Drunk" is a distinct state of being: "It’s like rich drunk. Either way, it’s legal to drive." It mocks the high-stakes, alcohol-fueled "networking" culture where million-dollar decisions are made at 2:00 AM over expensive scotch.

Liz: "Jack, you’re drunk."

Jack: "Well, it’s business drunk. It’s like rich drunk; either way, it’s legal to drive."

9. Power clashing: The deliberate act of wearing two mismatched patterns—like a striped tie with a checkered shirt—to signal to your peers that you are too successful to care about "rules." It’s the sartorial equivalent of a middle finger to the help.

Liz: "Jack, your tie is clashing with your shirt."

Jack: "It’s called power clashing, Lemon, and I do it because I can."

10. Backward Overflow Used to describe a situation where a project is so disastrously managed that it starts costing money in ways that shouldn't even be physically possible. It’s the perfect term for when corporate logic breaks down and starts eating itself.

Jack: "Since Tracy’s arrival, the show is up in key demographics for driving male viewership and we're effectively synergizing backward overflow."

Liz: "That doesn't mean anything."

Jack: "It means I’m getting a bonus and you’re firing ten percent of your staff. Keep up, Lemon."
