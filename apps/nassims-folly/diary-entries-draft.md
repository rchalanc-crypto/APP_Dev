## Entry: The Hill
**In-world date:** Day 52 — The Subida
**Author:** J.D. Morgoone
**Keeper's note:** Yes

### J.D.M. body

Dear Roberto,

I have driven the hill.

The hill in question is a mountain road of some character, recommended to me by a man at the petrol station who described it as "exciting." He was not wrong. The vehicle I selected — a Subaru of considerable modification, sourced through channels I will not detail — proved responsive in ways I had not entirely anticipated.

The first section went well. I am confident of this. The second section also went well, in the sense that the vehicle remained on the road throughout, which I understand to be the primary objective.

On the third section there was a moment of recalibration. The Keeper, who had been recording elapsed time on her clipboard rather than offering encouragement, suggested at this point that she take a turn.

I agreed. Partly out of generosity. Partly because the Subaru was making a sound I did not recognise.

She has asked me not to describe what happened next. I will respect this.

I will say only that we returned the vehicle undamaged and that the man at the petrol station, upon our return, spoke at some length to the Keeper and did not speak to me at all.

The hill is magnificent. I recommend it without reservation.

— J.D. Morgoone, Chronicler to the Expedition

### Keeper's note

The Captain completed the first two sections at an average speed of 34 km/h. The posted minimum for competitive runs is 60 km/h. I have not shared this with him.

On the third section the Captain entered the hairpin in third gear. The correct gear is second. The resulting correction took 11 seconds and involved a brief excursion onto the verge. The verge sustained no damage. The Captain's account omits the verge.

My run: 4 minutes, 12 seconds. The Captain's run: 9 minutes, 47 seconds, excluding the verge incident, which I am counting.

The man at the petrol station asked if I had competed before. I said no. He did not appear to believe me. We did not pursue it.

The Subaru is fine. I checked.

## Entry: The Course Respected Me
**In-world date:** Day 4 — Golf del Sur
**Author:** J.D. Morgoone
**Keeper's note:** Yes

### J.D.M. body

Dear Roberto,

I have played golf.

Golf del Sur received us this morning with the deference one would expect of a course that has hosted European Tour qualifying events. I arrived with a full set of clubs, borrowed from a man at the clubhouse who was leaving and seemed relieved to be rid of them.

The first hole went well. I placed the ball on the tee with authority. The drive was powerful and technically in the correct direction, though it found the rough through no fault of my own — the wind off the Atlantic at this course is capricious and does not respect the fundamentals.

Holes two through seven I will summarise as a period of adjustment.

On the eighth hole I achieved what my caddy described as "good contact." He has a conservative vocabulary. I have chosen to interpret this generously.

The Keeper accompanied us. She had not played before. On the third hole she asked me to explain the grip. I explained the grip. On the fourth hole she birdied. I have looked up what this means.

She played the back nine without the caddy's assistance and finished before me, which I attribute to her not pausing to appreciate the views, which are genuinely exceptional at this course and merit reflection.

My score is not relevant to this account.

I will return to the course. The course is not finished with me.

— J.D. Morgoone, Chronicler to the Expedition

### Keeper's note

The Captain's score was 127. I have been asked not to include this. I am including it for accuracy. Par is 72.

My score was 81. I had not played before. The Captain has played, by his own account, "several times over the years in various capacities." I did not ask him to clarify the capacities.

The caddy approached me on the 14th hole and asked if I would be interested in a lesson series. I explained I was only here for a fortnight. He gave me his card anyway. I have kept it.

The views are exceptional. The Captain is correct about this. It is the only element of his account I can fully endorse.

Tomorrow I am going kitesurfing. El Médano is reporting 18 knots from the northeast and a 1.2 metre swell. This is more useful information than a handicap.

## Entry: A Sport Built for Me
**In-world date:** Day 8 — The Property Court
**Author:** J.D. Morgoone
**Keeper's note:** Yes

### J.D.M. body

Dear Roberto,

The property has a pickleball court. I cannot overstate the significance of this discovery.

Pickleball, for those who have not yet encountered it, is a sport of precision, strategy, and considerable athleticism, played with a paddle and a perforated ball on a court of specific dimensions. I took to it immediately. The paddle felt natural in my hand. This is the sign of a natural.

I organised a round-robin. The Keeper declined initially, citing paperwork. I persuaded her on the grounds that the paperwork would still be there afterward and the afternoon would not.

She agreed with the qualifier that she had never played. I told her it would not matter. I was being reassuring. I was not expecting to be correct.

She beat me 11–3 in the first game.

In the second game she beat me 11–4. I want it noted that I won four points. I played those four points very well.

She returned to her paperwork afterward. She did not appear to have exerted herself particularly. She was not breathing hard. I was breathing somewhat hard. This is because I play with full commitment.

Pickleball is a magnificent sport. I intend to practise.

— J.D. Morgoone, Chronicler to the Expedition

### Keeper's note

I had not played pickleball before. The rules took approximately four minutes to learn. The Captain explained them to me; his explanation contained two errors, which I identified during play rather than beforehand to avoid delaying the start.

Game 1: 11–3. Game 2: 11–4. The four points in game 2 were legitimate. The Captain played them well. I have told him this. He has written it down somewhere, I believe.

The sport is straightforward. The ball moves slowly enough to read clearly and the court is small enough that positioning is the primary variable. The Captain positions himself in the centre regardless of where the ball is going. This is not a strategy. I have not told him this.

Glenn has asked me to play tomorrow morning. I have said yes.

The Captain has been practising his serve against the wall for forty minutes. The wall is not responding.

## Entry: I Have Soared. Technically.
**In-world date:** Day 23 — The Wing
**Posting note:** Post ~1 month before trip or during

### J.D.M. body

Dear Roberto,

I have acquired a wing.

Not a paraglider in the traditional sense — those are for men of patience
and thermals and long afternoons. This is a Moustache. A Flare Moustache,
to be precise, 18 metres of engineered sail that sits somewhere between a
kite and a paraglider and answers to neither category with full enthusiasm.

The Keeper has one. She did not mention this. I discovered it when she
returned from the cliffs at Tenesar with sand on her boots and the look of
someone who has done something very satisfying and sees no reason to
elaborate.

I asked if I might try it.

She assessed the wind — fourteen knots from the north — and said the
conditions were, in her view, appropriate for an introduction. This should
have told me something. The word "introduction" carries implications I did
not fully consider at the time.

I will say this: the cliff is very high. The lava below it is very black.
The wing, once inflated, has opinions about direction that are not always
aligned with one's own. I spent the first twelve minutes largely in
negotiation.

In the final eight minutes something resolved. The wing found its line.
The cliff ran beneath me. The Atlantic was below and the sky was above and
for exactly eight minutes I understood what the Keeper finds out there
every morning before anyone else is awake.

I did not attempt the barrel roll. The Keeper had demonstrated the barrel
roll. I made a note about the barrel roll for a future occasion.

— J.D. Morgoone, Chronicler to the Expedition

### Keeper's note

Wind: 14 knots, NNE. Conditions: suitable for introduction.

I fly the 13m in these conditions. The Captain was on the 18m. This is
correct. The 18m requires less wind to fly and is more forgiving on launch.
It is also slower. I did not say slower.

Launch attempt 1: the Captain walked forward into the wing instead of
backwards. The wing did not launch. No damage.

Launch attempt 2: the Captain asked me to explain backwards again.
I explained backwards again.

Launch attempt 3: successful. The Captain flew for 20 minutes along the
cliff line. His line was conservative but consistent. He did not approach
the lava. This was the correct decision.

The barrel roll he mentions: I performed two. They were not for
demonstration purposes. I perform them because the site is excellent for
it and I had time. The Captain has interpreted this as a lesson. I have
not corrected him.

He has since asked where one acquires an 18m Moustache. I have sent him
three links. He has not mentioned the instruction course. I have not
mentioned the instruction course either.

We will see.
